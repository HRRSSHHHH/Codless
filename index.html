Why I Did This?

Everyone kept shouting, “Learn AI before it takes your job.” So I stopped scrolling and started building. I used AI to design, code, animate, and launch this entire website—no devs, no hand-written code. It’s not perfect, but it’s real. And it proves that experimenting beats overthinking.

Let's See How We Do It!

Step 1 Inspiration (The Spark)
It started with a what if…
One evening, I found myself thinking — what if I could build a full website without writing a single line of code? Not a landing page template. A real, functioning, animated site. Everyone's talking about AI taking jobs. I wanted to flip it — and see what I could build with it. That moment became this project.
Step 2 Curiosity (Ask the right questions)
So... how do I even begin?
I opened ChatGPT and treated it like my creative co-founder. I asked: — What’s the goal of this site? — What problem am I solving? — What should the structure look like? It responded with a rough information architecture, a goal statement, and even suggested how the site could feel. That’s when it clicked: AI isn’t just a tool—it’s a thinking partner.
Step 3 Creative Flow (Imagine the Feel)
What should this feel like?
I wanted more than just structure—I wanted vibe. So I asked ChatGPT to find the core emotion my site should evoke, then built everything around that: Suggested keywords that matched the emotion. Picked typography styles for personality. Chose colors that visually reflect the emotion. That one prompt became the foundation for the entire visual system.
Step 4 Exploration (Research Like a Builder)
Steal like a strategist.
I used Perplexity AI to find the best websites related to my concept—not to copy, but to understand what works. I asked it to show me: Top sites in the same space. Their layout patterns, components, and UX flows. Useful assets I could borrow or remix. It wasn't research for the sake of it. I was building a toolbox.
Step 5 Playfulness + Taste (Make It Visual)
Smashing the spacebar.
I took my primary and secondary colors, plugged them into Coolors.co, and started smashing the spacebar. Every refresh brought a new vibe. Once it clicked, I locked in 4 core tones: Primary Secondary Neutral Light Neutral Dark Then I built out tints and shades for each—turning colors into a usable system, not just a palette.
Step 6 Resourcefulness (Remix the Greats)
Steal smart, Remix it better.
I picked my top 3 reference websites that i got from my research in Perplexity AI—the ones that nailed layout, clarity, or vibe. Then I asked Grok AI to break them down. I ranked them (favorite to least), and told Grok to use the 60/30/10 rule: 60% from the best 30% from the second 10% from the third.
Step 7 Clarity (Brief the Machine)
You get what you brief for.
Before letting Grok generate anything, I fed it the full story: The goal of the site, The problem it solves, The rough information architecture. I treated it like a creative partner, not just a code generator. With the right inputs, Grok knew exactly what to build—and how to structure it.
Step 8 Momentum (Generate the First Draft)
Voila, a website
Grok took the brief and gave me the full index.html file—structured, styled, and ready to build on. It wasn’t perfect (nothing is at first), but it gave me momentum. I wasn’t staring at a blank screen anymore. I was staring at something real.
Step 8(Alternative) Designer Route (Build It Yourself)
Control freaks option.
If you think better in pixels than prompts, this step’s for you. Instead of generating code, I opened Figma and created a rough layout using Auto Layout. Why? Because tools like Figma to Code plugins or AI helpers can convert that into clean HTML + CSS. No guessing. No guessing divs. Just design-first, code-later flow.
Step 9 Trust (Meet Your AI Dev Partner)
I didn’t hire a dev.
I dropped my index.html file into Cursor AI, started a new project, and selected the folder like any normal repo. From there, Cursor took over like a clean-freak senior developer: Separated HTML, CSS, JS into best-practice folders Organized everything into a proper file structure Let me tweak anything — just by asking I wasn’t editing code. I was having a conversation with it.
Step 10 Empowerment (Talk to Your Code )
Started coding Talking.
With Cursor AI, I didn’t need to Google syntax or dig through Stack Overflow. I just said things like: “Center this hero section” “Make the navbar sticky on scroll” “Change this animation timing to 0.3s” And it updated the actual code. The more I talked to it, the cleaner and more custom my site became.
Step 11 Problem-Solving (Custom Elements = New Prompts)
If not one than another
Sometimes, Cursor couldn’t pull off what I wanted — a complex interaction, a micro-animation, or a custom component. So I took the idea to Grok AI or Gemini or Perplexity. I gave it context, a visual reference, and asked for a working code snippet. Once it looked good in preview, I brought it back to Cursor and had it integrate the code into my site. One prompt here. One there. And just like that — it worked.
Step 12 Satisfaction (It’s Ready to Launch)
No devs. No drag-and-drop.
After a few rounds of tweaks, fixes, and fun hacks, the site was ready. Everything was structured, responsive, and fully mine—built with nothing but prompts, curiosity, and persistence. From a blank idea to a full, live website—this was never about replacing anyone. It was about proving that designers can build too now.
